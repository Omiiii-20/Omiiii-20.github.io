I got into embedded systems not from a textbook, but from wanting to understand how code physically controls things — how a single line of C can pulse a GPIO, drive a motor, or send a packet over a CAN bus at exactly the right moment.

During my final year, I interned at RedNerds, the ODM division of Evobi Automation — working on real embedded hardware and communication protocols in a product development environment. That experience of writing code that runs on actual silicon, under a real deadline, shaped how I think about firmware.

After graduating, I went deeper through intensive training at Emertxe — building real-time communication systems, implementing POSIX threads, and writing low-level software in Linux. Every project pushed me to think in terms of memory, timing, and hardware constraints.

I've also presented a research paper on an embedded wearable and built systems ranging from a custom Linux shell to a LoRa-based IoT monitor on ARM Cortex-M4. I want to work on systems where every byte and every clock cycle counts.
